Part One

The Software

Phase 1 — Active Now

Sarvayu is not a fitness app. It is a biological orchestrator. It takes everything known about a person — their chronotype, their cultural food patterns, their stress signals, their biomarkers, their sleep rhythms — and sequences their day in the order that their biology actually wants.

The intelligence engine is called Ayurkram — from Sanskrit Ayu (life) and Kram (sequence). Ayurkram reads the user's biology and produces a daily sequence: when to eat, when to train, when to recover, when to rest. Timed to the individual, not to a clock.

What Makes It Different

Cultural intelligence at the core. Sarvayu knows the difference between a South Indian eating curd rice and a Punjabi eating dal makhni. It knows that Navratri fasting changes the eating window. 30+ food cultures, each with real meal data, snack swaps, and recovery foods. This is not a feature. It is the foundation.

No shame, no punishment. Sarvayu's behavioral twin predicts when the user is likely to skip a workout or stress-eat — and intervenes before it happens, not after. When a heavy meal is logged, the next three dinners silently recalibrate. The user never sees a warning. They just see excellent meals the next three days.

Proactive, not reactive. By 7 AM, the Ayurkram plan exists. The user opens the app and the day is already organized. One readiness score. One big thing to do. The sequence, ready.

Part Two

The Device

Phase 2 — Research Underway

Every wearable on the market was designed for Western bodies, Western climates, and Western price points. None of them were built for India. None of them measure what actually matters for the Indian population — cortisol variation across different work cultures, the metabolic response to a rice-dominant diet, the impact of 45°C summer heat on recovery metrics.

Sarvayu's device will be built for India from the first prototype.

Generation 1 In Development
Foundational Biometrics
  • Heart rate variability — non-invasive readiness signal
  • SpO2 — blood oxygen, sleep quality, altitude adaptation
  • Skin temperature — hormonal state, illness onset, ovulation
  • Accelerometer — movement, sedentary patterns, sleep stages
  • Galvanic skin response — real-time stress, emotional load

Hormonal Intelligence

  • Cycle phase estimation — enabling cycle-synced plans
  • Testosterone proxy from morning HRV baseline

  • Hydration index from skin impedance
  • Vitamin D proxy from UV exposure across the day

The majority of India's chronic disease burden is lifestyle-modifiable. Not inevitable. The result of misaligned eating windows, chronic stress without recovery, and the displacement of traditional Indian food wisdom by processed food culture.

The Indian healthcare system is built to treat disease after it appears. There is no infrastructure for biological intelligence at the individual level — the kind of continuous, personalized, culturally-rooted guidance that could prevent disease before it begins.

The Sarvayu National Health Proposal

Government of India procures Sarvayu devices and platform access through existing health infrastructure

◈
Sarvayu wearable device Procured at government scale pricing — under ₹800 per unit. Distributed through PMJAY, ASHA workers, Jan Arogya clinics.
◈
Platform access at ₹0 Subsidized subscription for BPL cardholders. Full Ayurkram plan generated on day one.
◈
Weekly biological reports Shared with each citizen's primary care provider. Integration with ABHA health records.
◈
India's first national biological baseline database Anonymized, consented biometric data from millions of users — available to ICMR and international researchers.

Questions This Database Will Answer

  • How does cortisol variance differ between agricultural workers in Vidarbha and tech workers in Bengaluru?
  • What is the actual hormonal impact of Ramadan fasting on Indian Muslim women across different BMI ranges?
  • How does the traditional South Indian breakfast pattern affect insulin sensitivity compared to the North Indian pattern?
  • What is the relationship between social jetlag and cardiovascular risk in India's shift-working population?
